*updated 03/17/26*

The first post on this site is about the book that began my fascination with early aviation. It is the first edition of Thomas Walker's *A Treatise Upon the Art of Flying*, printed in Hull in 1810. It is unfortunate that the frontispiece depicting Walker's flying machine is soiled and has been repaired in this copy. Nevertheless, this copy is particularly interesting because it has been inscribed by Walker to a Tho. Jagues. Auction records show that this exact copy went up for sale in 1986 and sold for about $550.

Little was known about Walker's life until recently. The title page lists his occupation as a portrait painter, and according to the Hull History Centre, there is evidence that Walker was a painter of miniature portraits beginning in at least 1801. In the newspaper and advertisements of the time, he is often listed as "T. Walker." The Centre provides the following information about where Walker lived in Hull between 1801 and 1807:

"He lived at the premises of Mr Blanchard's, Dock Street, Hull in June 1801.  By November 1801 he had moved to reside at the premises of Mr Mowatt, a stationer in Market Place, Hull. He supplied lessons on "Drawing, according to the true principles of Perspective". By 1807, he had moved to the premises of Roger Walker, a Grocer and Tea Dealer in Whitefriargate, Hull. This time he is listed as a "portrait painter". Later that year, is listed as supplying Portrait Paintings and Miniatures."

The Hull History Centre provided the following excerpts from magazines and newspapers suggesting that Walker was a traveling lecturer who gave lectures on astronomy and aerostation. In the December 13, 1826, issue of *Bury and Norwich Post*, a "Mr. Walker" is said to be delivering a lecture on astronomy, and it is added that "of those who have a taste for the extraordinary Productions of Art, Mr. W. will also give a full and clear Explanation of the Principles of Mechanic Aerostation, or the Art of navigating the Air, by Mechanical Means alone ! Experiments will be shown to demonstrate the Practicability of this wonderful Art !" The article also states that "A TREATISE on MECHANICAL AEROSTATION by Mr. Walker, may be had of him". The May 10, 1828, issue of *Durham and County Advertiser* mentions that a "Treatise by Mr Walker, on Mechanical Aerostation" may be had. The paper also states that Walker was an honorary member of the Philosophical Society of London and the Maidstone Philosophical Society. The *South Eastern Gazette*, on November 13, 1827, reported that a Mr. Walker gave a lecture on astronomy at the Maidstone Philosophical Society and that he had previously given a lecture on aerostation with a demonstration using a model. In the July 13, 1835, issue of *Liverpool Albion*, Walker is mentioned as being a lecturer of astronomy, and it is noted that he had several items stolen, including a number of curious insects. The article also mentions that Walker had a daughter. The *Liverpool Echo* mentions Walker by his first name in its issue from April 17, 1846. It states that Walker, "as a lecturer, has been known for great number of years, and appreciated, not only for his knowledge of the science, but also for the clear and simple manner in which illustrates, the different subjects brought before his hearers." It also mentions a "concave orrery" that Walker used for his lectures.

J. E. Hodgson's *The History of Aeronautics in Great Britain* contains a footnote stating that "Walker is referred to by a writer in the *Mechanics' Mag.* (vol. xiii, 1830, p. 350) as a 'gentleman of great talent', unassuming but communicative, save in the presence of 'the critic, the wit, or the more heavy part of mankind, who are too tiresome to instruct.'" Walker's treatise itself offers little insight into his life. However, it is evident that Walker was little more than an amateur hobbyist when it came to aeronautics, at least when the first edition was printed. His approach to mechanical flight came from dissecting and studying birds and the mechanisms that allowed them to fly. According to him, only two things were needed for sustained flight: large enough wings and enough strength to power them. His idea for a flying machine essentially consisted of planks supporting a chair with wings that could be controlled by a lever to make the wings flap, thus allowing the machine to rise in the air.

There is evidence that Walker built at least one prototype. Hodgson's *History* states that a witness saw the machine Walker had constructed but that he was "unable to raise himself from the platform on which the car was placed." The picture below shows Walker's flying machine from the first edition. It is likely that the machine Walker attempted to demonstrate was based on the second edition of this work.

Copies of the first edition of his work were taken to mainland Europe and America. The first American edition appeared in 1814 and was reprinted in 1816, showing that there was considerable interest in this work.

In the second edition, printed in Bristol in 1831, Walker updated the design for his flying machine. The shape and number of wings changed, and Walker suggested the possibility of adding hydrogen gas in the wings to aid in flight. In this edition, Walker also noted the experiments in mechanical flight taking place across France and America.

There is another, undated edition that was printed sometime in the early 1820s. The title page states that it is the second edition, but given that the contents and frontispiece are identical to the first edition, it is more appropriate to say that this is a second printing of the first edition. The title of this printing has been changed from the first edition to match that of the second edition. The ETH Library in Zurich also holds [a copy of this printing](https://www.e-rara.ch/zut/doi/10.3931/e-rara-15922), and their copy is inscribed by Walker with "sincere respect." The inscription is dated August 12, 1827.

{{< figure src="second printing.jpg" title="Title page of the undated second printing, provided by Lok Man Rare Books. Their copy contains an ownership inscription dated 1822." width="550">}}

The Aeronautical Society of Great Britain reprinted this work in The Aeronautical Classics, in which both editions of this work appear. In the foreword, the following is written about Walker: "His sole claims to immortality are the two editions of his pamphlet upon flying, which exhibit him as an earnest amateur with a hobby and some small conceit of himself. He fumbles with great truths: knows much, but fails to order his knowledge, and hovers on the brink of great achievements in a way that is quite exasperating. For these reasons his writings are, perhaps, the most fascinating of all those of the pioneers."

All of the editions of this work from the early 19th century are rare. It seems that the first edition was hard to find even a couple decades after its initial publication. In volume vii of *Mechanics' Magazine* from 1827, which contains excerpts from the first edition, H. Bell writes the following about this work:

"In the book-case of a deceased friend I found, several years ago, a curious pamphlet, which I have every reason to suppose is scarce, or perhaps was printed for the author's friends alone."

Bell goes on to note that he tried making inquiries about the author while passing through Hull but was unable to locate him. He did, however, hear about Walker's plan to add a triangular parachute to his flying machine for not only safety reasons but also to provide additional lift.

{{< figure src="parachute.jpg" title="Proposed parachute to Walker's flying machine" width="550">}}

Some of the pioneers of aviation in the late-19th and early-20th centuries recognized Walker's contributions and decided to inlude this work in several magazines and publications, sometimes in abridged form. It was reprinted in the 1895 edition of *The Aeronautical Annual*, and a stand-alone reprint printed by The Aeronautical Society of Great Britain appeared in 1910 as *The Art of Flying*. It also appeared in the *Twelfth Annual Report of the Aeronautical Society of Great Britain for the Year 1877*. It was noted in this report that a copy of the first edition was lent to the Secretary by Edward Bannister of Grimsby and that "The publication of this Pamphlet was a matter of history, but it was not known whether a copy was in existence."
