"The Mechanics of Flight and 'Aspiration'" was a paper first read by Arthur Mellen Wellington before the International Conference on Aerial Navigation, which was held in Chicago from August 1-4, 1893. The paper was then reprinted in the October 12, 1893, issue of *Engineering News*, which Wellington was co-owner and editor of. The byline indicates that Wellington was a member of the American Society of Civil Engineers.

In this paper, Wellington discusses the mechanics of bird flight and puts forth twelve propositions as part of his theory. The propositions are "so simple and so evidently true that might almost be accepted as axiomatic." He also discusses the idea of "aspiration," or "rising against the wind by power taken from the wind." The propositions are summarized below.

<ol>
  <li>"At no time and in no degree does any bird use its wings in normal flight as a means of propulsion."</li>
  <li>"This buoyant force is obtained in two ways, either by (1) flapping the wings in air ... or (2) exposing the wings as aeroplanes to air which is moving relatively to the bird, or (3) in part by both."</li>
  <li>"The propelling force of the bird is at all times either (1) a process of sliding downhill upon the air ... or (2) what is a precise mechanical equivalent, though reversed in direction, sliding uphill on the air ... when buoyancy is made to exceed gravity."</li>
  <li>"The bird maintains its stability transversely in two ways ... by having the center of buoyancy considerably above the center of gravity ... and by the upward inclination of the wings in soaring ... so that any tendency to tip sidewise is checked by an increase of the supporting area on the descending side and a decrease on the other side."</li>
  <li>"The longitudinal stability of the bird is maintained by an action of forces strictly analogous to those which maintain the stability of the old-fashioned steelyard, the tail having, by insensible modifications of its angle to the body, the same function as the moving weight of the steelyard to maintain stability."</li>
  <li>"It is certain that in the living bird this slight motion of the tail for balancing is effected by an unconscious reflex action of the nerves and muscles."</li>
  <li>"The sustention of the bird in the air when soaring depends primarily on three facts: (1) The air has mass and is in immediate contact with the bird, so that it is impossible for the bird to fall at all until and except as it causes the air immediately below it to move with it; (2) it is impossible for the bird to cause this air to move downward any faster than gravity alone would cause it to fall in vacuo, or even quite so fast; and (3) the velocity with which a body begins to fall is almost inconceivably slow."</li>
  <li>"To derive any sustention from the back part of the wings, which rest only on disturbed air, the back of the wing should dip downward so as to be concave below, so as to follow up, and get a reaction from the descending air."</li>
  <li>"The concavity of the wings has another useful effect for maintaining stability. The buoyant force is applied a little in front of the center of gravity, thus tending to lift the head up and the tail down. By the upward reaction against the back of the wings an opposite rotating force is created, tending to throw the tail up and the head down."</li>
  <li>"Buoyancy in soaring appears to be obtained by having the plane of the wings at a very slight angle with the 'body-plane,' as we may term it, which latter may be considered to consist of the tail of a plane equivalent in its effect on the direction of motion to the body of the bird."</li>
  <li>"The bird's loss by atmospheric friction in moving through the air is probably so small as to be almost negligible, measured in feet of fall per mile necessary to maintain a given speed indefinitely."</li>
  <li>"A body moving through space in any direction has in it a certain fixed amount of energy for a given velocity, which is such as would suffice to lift the body through the height through which it would have to fall."</li>
</ol>

This copy was extracted from the complete issue of October 12, 1893. Included with this extract is another extract from the October 26, 1893, issue, which contains an objection by Professor Irving Potter Church of Cornell University. Potter's objection is that "a bird which suddenly turns into the wind from going with the wind must be considered as in effect going around a curved track ... fixed in the air, and so surrendering back to the air whatever energy it may have taken from it." Wellington says this is not how a bird flies through the air. Rather, a bird's "nature enables it to change its direction of motion by ricocheting against the air somewhat as a billiard ball does against a cushion or by moving in a curved path as a ship may in water." Wellington urges Potter to watch the "true soaring birds," such as the carrion crow or buzzard, to learn about how they manuever through the air. Two other correspondents, one from Binghamton, New York, and a George Y. Wisner, also give objections, to which Wellington provides responses.
