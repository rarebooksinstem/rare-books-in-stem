"L'Exploration par Fus&#233;es de la tr&#232;s haute atmosph&#232;re et la Possibilit&#233; des Voyages Interplan&#233;taires" was a lecture delivered by Robert Esnault-Pelterie before the Soci&#233;t&#233; astronomique de France on June 8, 1927. This lecture was well-received, and the audience "frequently interrupted it with applause" (*From Astronautics to Cosmonautics* by Mike Gruntman).

{{< figure src="title.jpg" title="Title page" width="550" class="left">}}

The preface states that Esnault-Pelterie first studied this probem in 1907 but did not publish his ideas until 1912, when he gave a lecture in front of the Societ&#233; fran&#231;aise de Physique (French Physical Society). While the problem was far from being solved, this work provides an important discussion on propulsion devices for space travel. In the introduction, Esnault-Pelterie states that, though he cannot recall the exact date, his initial ideas on the subject go back to when he read Jules Vernes' *De la Terre &#224; la Lune*. He mentions that a similar idea was put forth by Dr. Andr&#233; Bing in 1911; a patent from June 10 of that year (Belgian patent no. 236377) was taken out for "an apparatus intended to allow the exploration of the upper regions of the atmosphere." Furthermore, Bisa had had conversations on the matter with Edouard Belin several years prior. However, as Bing mentioned to Esnault-Pelterie in 1913, he wanted to secure the right of priority with his patent and so did not conduct in-depth calculations. In America, Robert H. Goddard carried out theoretical calculations between 1912 and 1913 and conducted experiments on rockets in 1915 and 1916 at Clark University in Worcester, Massachusetts. Goddard studied the problem from the point of view of sending a projectile loaded with powder to the moon and observing it with a telescope. Esnault-Pelterie, on the other hand, was considering the conditions necessary to send a vehicle with passengers from one planet to another and safely return the passengers to Earth.

This paper is divided into five chapters and a conclusion section. The chapters cover i) rocket motion in a vacuum, ii) rocket motion in the air, iii) use of rockets to explore very high regions in the atmosphere and for interplanetary travel, iv) conditions necessary to transport living beings on a vehicle traveling through space, and v) scientific interest in visiting other worlds. Esnault-Pelterie concludes from his studies that travel to the moon is more possible than he had first imagined. However, there are still unknown risks and problems to consider, such as the stability of large quantities of atomic hydrogen, the ability of nozzles to withstand large temperatures, the weight of the equipment needed to sustain life onboard the vehicle, and how living beings can cope with "the complete suppression of the gravitational field."

This copy is inscribed by Esnault-Pelterie to a "dear friend" sometime in March of 1928. It is bound in the original red printed wrappers. A duplicate copy of pages 49 through 64 is tipped in at the end.
