This short pamphlet of five pages contains an address that Jules-Fran&#231;ois Dupuis-Delcourt gave in front of l'Acad&#233;mie des sciences in Paris in 1850. In it, he discusses some of the technical aspects of aerial navigation and the future possibilities of aeronautics. He begins by saying that the balloon was a great invention, one destined for "powerful and useful applications." However, the application of aerostatics to the general needs of humanity are no longer be considered a problem of art or science but that of money and time. 

He then provides an example of a spherical aerostat 50 meters in diameter with a crew of 25 men. With sufficient provisions, it could stay in the air for weeks or even months. He estimates its weight to be 75 tons. Using a vessel like this ship, it would be possible to stay in the air long enough to study the atmosphere for future aeronautical endeavors. Furthermore, this type of vessel could be used for exploration. He surmises that a trip from Paris to the polar regions would take five days, and it would be possible to determine the existence of a Northwest Passage. Traveling through the air would avoid the obstacles faced by ships, such as ice and other obstacles on the ground. Dupuis-Delcourt ends his address by saying that he approached the government as early as 1829 for help with his ideas. He also asks the Academy for approval and its knowledge on the subject.

This copy was once held at the Library of Congress but was deaccessioned because it was a duplicate copy.
