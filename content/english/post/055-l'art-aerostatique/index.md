This short book was written by an anonymous author, and while it appears in some bibliographies (most notably, Gaston Tissandier's *Bibliographie a&#233;ronautique* and Paul Brockett's *Bibliography of Aeronautics*), no description of the book's contents is provided. The book begins with a discussion on the theory of aerostats and how objects can rise in the atmosphere because of the action of a bouyant force. The author does not dwell on what knowledge the ancients possessed of aerial navigation, leaving that "conjecture to the scholars," though it is strange to him that studying soap bubbles or rain clouds did not lead to the invention of aerostats sooner.

The author begins the historical survey by discussing the Montgolfier Brothers. In 1782, the brothers made small bags of paper or silk that they filled with hydrogen gas. The bags, carefully sealed, rose and remained in the air for "considerable periods of time." However, the gas was expensive, and the materials caused it to leak. They began using rarified air using fire, and a balloon made of canvas and with a capacity of about 851,000 liters was able to rise to a height of 200 or 300 meters. On June 5, 1783, the brothers conducted an experiment in Annonay with a spherical balloon made of canvas. It was twelve meters in diameter and contained nearly 900,000 liters of air. It weighed 250 kilograms. Two men were sufficient to assemble it and fill it with air. Once a signal was given, the eight men holding it let go, and the balloon rose to a height of about 500 meters. After ten minutes, it descended slowly.

This experiment was repeated in Paris by Robert and Charles, who made a balloon four meters in diameter and filled it with hydrogen. This balloon remained hovering at a height of 100 feet, and it was said to arouse such curiosity that "it was necessary to post guards on foot and horseback ... to control the crowd of onlookers." The author describes the ascents of several others, including Pilatre de Rozier, Pierre Testu-Brissy (who ascended in a horse), &#201;tienne-Gaspard Robert ("Robertson"), Louis-Bernard Guyton de Morveau, Charles de Virly, Jean-Baptiste Biot, Joseph Louis Gay-Lussac, Jean-Pierre and Sophie Blanchard, and Andr&#233;-Jacques Garnerin. The only plate in this book shows the various types of balloons used by these aeronauts and others.

There are some other descriptions of interest. The first women ascended in a balloon made by &#201;tienne Montgolfier in the gardens of R&#233;veillon. The women were the Marquise de Montalembert, the Countess de Montalembert, the Countess de Podenas, and Mademoiselle de Lagarde. They were accompanied by the Marquis de Montalembert and M. Artaud de Bellevue. This flight was led by Pilatre de Rozier. It is said that Queen Marie-Antoinette and the Princess of Polignac had intended to join this flight but decided against it based on the King's advice. Another woman aeronaut, opera singer &#201;lisabeth Thible, performed "a magnificent ascent" in Lyon on June 4, 1874, to mark a visit by King Gustav III of Sweden.

Another notable ascent, "one that marks an era in the history of ballooning," is that of Blanchard and Dr. John Jeffries. On January 7, 1785, they crossed the English Channel, and this earned Blanchard the nickname "Don Quixote de le Mancha." Jacob Degen, the watchmaker from Vienna, is also mentioned. In 1812, Degen "pompously announced" a device that would allow him to stay aloft and that he could steer through the air. Wings and an inclined plane with the aid of a balloon were supposed to help with this. However, the experiment on the Champ de Mars failed, and "the angry crowd tore the machine to pieces."

The other sections or chapters in this book include travel impressions describing the ascent from an aeronaut's perspective, various methods used to construct aerostats, and "the art of flying" (navigation through the air). In the latter section, there is a discussion on bird flight.

There is a faint inscription on the title page, though it is difficult to make out the words.
