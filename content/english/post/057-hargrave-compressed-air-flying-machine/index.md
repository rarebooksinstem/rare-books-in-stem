Volume 24 of *Journal and Proceedings of the Royal Society of New South Wales* contains two papers by Lawrence Hargrave on his flying machine. The first paper, "On a Compressed-air Flying-machine," was read before the Royal Society of New South Wales on June 4, 1890, in which he "maintains that this is the first practical demonstration that a machine can, and does fly by the simple flapping of wings." Hargrave discusses the engine and the nature of the wings, which must take into account the "disturbances set up in the air." Other parts, such as the air-pump and starting gear, of the flying machine are discussed. To deal with landings, a stick was secured under the compressed-air receiver and projected sixteen inches past the engine so that, when the flying machine landed, the stick broke and minimized damage to the engine and receiver. Though there were seven wrecks, the machine sustained "no material damage." Some of the wrecks were believed to have been caused by a high center of gravity.

This paper is accompanied by three fold-out plates. The first plate shows a scale model of this machine during its seventh trial. The second plate shows the compressed-air engine. The third plate shows the engine of a screw-driven machine that Hargrave also tested. He concluded that "screw machines should have the blades set parallel to the screw shaft, and allow the pitch to be automatically adjusted by torsion."

At the end of the paper, two comments were given. The first, by Mr. H. C. Russell, is as follows: "I am one of those who admire the ingenuity and perserverance displayed by Mr. Hargrave in his attempts to produce a perfect flying-machine. I congratulate him upon the production of those ingenious models intended to copy the methods followed by nature to produce motion in various animal organizations. I think it is greatly to his credit that he now willingly admits he finds after experiment that he can attain far better results with a simple form of mechanism than with that of a complex nature. He has introduced several important inventions in connection with his scheme, which in the case of most men would have been taken to the Patents Office without delay, but which he from pure love of science freely gives to the world as part of his flying-machine. I have much pleasure in proposing a vote of thanks to Mr. Hargrave for his interesting paper."

The President of the Royal Society, Dr. Charles Adolph Leibus, added the following: "Mr. Hargrave has my best wishes for the success of his experiments. It would be a very gratifying thing if a really practical flying-machine were invented by an Australian. They say the proof of the pudding is in the eating, and the success of this invention will be proved when we see him flying across to North Shore (Laughter). I wish him every success."

The second paper, "On the 74oz. Compressed-air Flying-machine," was read before the Society on December 3, 1890. Hargrave made a larger flying-machine and conducted two flights with it. In this paper, he discusses the changes he made to the engine components since the previous paper from June 4 and the results he obtained from his experiments. This paper is accompanied by four plates bound at the end of the volume.
