"Is a Flying Machine a Mechanical Possibility?" was written by an anonymous author and discusses the practical possibility of mechanical flight. As the author states, the question "has been answered, theoretically, in the affirmative many times; but it has never been practically answered except in the negative." The author adds: "Much as has been said, written, and done in the elucidation of this subject, it is astonishing how little has been to the purpose. The inventions which have from time to time been made and tried only to demonstrate their utter absurdity, have been for the most part constructed in apparent ignorance of the true principles involved; and those who have criticised these inventions and ridiculed them have shown, in a majority of instances, almost as much ignorance as those whose work they have condemned."

The author says that the first exhibition held by the Aeronautical Society [of Great Britain] in June of 1868 shows that interest in mechanical flight is increasing, not decreasing. Though steam engines have been considered inadequate because of their large weight compared to their power, Mr. Stringfellow won &#163;100 for a one-horsepower engine that weighed 16 lbs. The engine worked 300 revolutions per minute, and it took seven minutes to achieve a full working power of 100 lbs. The weight of the engine and boiler was 13 lbs. Without water and fuel, the weight of the engine, boiler, car, and propeller was 16 lbs. Given the area of the piston, the pressure in the cylinder, the length of the stroke, and the velocity of the piston, the power output was calculated to be 36,000 foot-pounds, where one horsepower "is reckoned at 33,000 foot-pounds" [per minute].

It is noted that Dr. Cornelius B. Fox translated M. de Lucy's paper "On the Flight of Birds, of Bats, and of Insects," which touches on aerial navigation. M. de Lucy's findings are that "in flying animals the extent of winged surface is always in inverse ratio to the weight of the creature." To support a man, a surface of 29 square yards, 8 square feet, and 14 square inches is required. Any supporting arms or beams need to have strength, rigidity, and lightness. "When a man can make a structure as strong, as rigid, as elastic, as light in proportion to bulk as a goose quill, the problem of flight will be nearly solved." Any lack of power in a man's arms may be overcome by using thighs and legs to drive the wings or flying apparatus.

The author ends by saying "What is now required, is a material combining greatest strength with least weight. We know of no such material now available for the purpose. We therefore conclude that until such materials are discovered man will not fly."
