Ernst Freiherr von Wechmar's *Zur Flugfrage*, printed in 1891, is a continutation of his previous writings on "flight technology" between 1886 and 1888. He states in the preface that his goal is to "provide a unified overview and offer the necessary explanations and additions to my main work on flight technology" and that his ideas on this topic "have matured considerably." As the title states, this book is Wechmar's way to offering suggestions to any contemporaries who are also interested in the question of flight. The preface was written in Weidling, a village located north of Vienna, and is dated end of September, 1891. Based on several statements in this book, it is evident that much of Wechmar's work went unnoticed and unappreciated during his time.

There are eight chapters in the book. The first deals with birds and how their plumage is an important component for flight. Wechmar states that the study of bird flight has not led to any successes in aeronautics. He first wrote about the importance of plumage in his earlier book *Wechmar's Flugtechnik*, though it received "relatively little attention and appreciation." Despite the plumage adding considerable weight and volume to the bird's body, it allows the bird to control itself during flight and regulate its speed. Some flying insects can also be used for inspiration for mechanical devices, especially when it comes to double wings and rigid vs. flexible wings.

The second chapter is about suggestions for different methodologies in the field of aeronautical engineering. The two approaches to flight are using balloons (i.e., lighter-than-air) and mechanical flight (i.e., heavier-than-air). Wechmar proposes a third approach that combines these two and lays the foundation for the remaining chapters.

The third chapter is on the theory of "personal flying." He notes that any flying machine cannot simply imitate the apparatus of birds or any other flying animal because technology cannot adequately replicate the aparatus. The fourth chapter contains a description of Wechmar's "parachute balloon" along with discussions on the lifting power of air and the best shape for a balloon. Wechmar's invention is based on the idea that a flat, wide body that allows air to penetrate it in the horizontal direction has a better lifting capacity than a traditional air sac. It is meant to serve as an accessory to his existing flying apparatus for preliminary flight tests. The parachute is filled with air and is unweighted so that "it rises like a feather at the slightest air movement." An illustration of this apparatus can be found in *Wechmar's Flugtechnik* and is not included in the present work. To prevent accidents during landing, air cushions filled with carbon or hydrogen are placed around a person's chest and legs. Wechmar ends the chapter by noting how testing with this apparatus should be conducted and what results should be expected.

The fifth chapter discusses the theory of dynamic airships, that is, airships that have a means of propulsion and can carry passengers. Wechmar's suggestion is that balloons are inappropriate for any airship that has its own means of propulsion because they can be uncontrollable based on the wind speed and direction. He recommends conducting experiments on personal flight (i.e., without passengers and active propulsion) because it will benefit dynamic flight.

The sixth chapter is on the systematics of aeronautics, which includes a discussion on airship design and construction. The seventh chapter contains another practical application, this time of Wechmar's "ship kite." An illustraton of this can be found in *Flugtechnik*. He notes that this kite alone does not possess any lifting capability and will most likely remain nothing more than a toy for children. However, if some sort of gas bubble were to be placed under the kite's frame, it could gain lifting capability. The final chapter contains concluding remarks, in which Wechmar says it is impossible for a single person to achieve the goal of flight and that he encourages collaboration in this area.

This book contains several ads for other works in aeronatics, including some by Wechmar. The verso of the front wrapper has ads for two aeronautical journals printed by W. H. K&#252;hl. The recto of the rear wrapper contains ads for two "well-preserved copies" of aeronautics books from 1886 and 1888. Finally, the rear wrapper contains information on the three books that make up *Wechmar's Flugtechnik*. They are offered bound in a single volume, and it is noted that the books are written not only for specialists but also for the scientifically educated. This particular copy comes from the Library of Congress.
