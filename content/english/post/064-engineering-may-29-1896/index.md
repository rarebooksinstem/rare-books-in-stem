"The Sailing Flight of Birds" is an article of about 1000 words that describes Samuel Langley's studies on wind and how birds take advantage of the energy stored in wind gusts. The author mentions Langley's paper "The Internal Work of the Wind," which was printed in *American Journal of Science* in 1894 and can be found in volume XLVII, issue 277 of that journal. Langley showed, when the wind blows with irregular gusts (i.e., non-uniform velocty), it stores a large amount of energy that can be used by birds for flight. The author of this article argues that any inclination of a bird's wings to take advantage of wind gusts happens automatically. The effect of the wind on a dandelion seed is used as a example to prove the author's point, where the sudden motion of the wind causes dandelion seeds to rise into the air (shown in the figure below). "The more such a seed is blown about the more it rises in the air," he writes. A bird, however, needs to adopt other means for lift as well, and this is done by repeating the "horizontal plane dropper" experiments conducted by Langley and discussed in his book *Experiments on Aerodynamics* (1891). There are two main results from that book. One is that a horizontal surface traveling in the horizontal direction at a high velocity will fall slower than simply falling vertically. The other is that the power required to sustain a body in horizontal is lower for high speeds compared to low speeds.

Birds use the same principles during flight, slowing their descent in a uniform wind by circling round and round at considerable speed and deriving lift from gusts of wind "on account of the increased bouyancy due to their horizontal velocity." The author ends the article by saying that the flight of birds is less a "physical miracle of nature" (as Langley calls it) and more "a simple and natural result of the laws which Professor Langley himself has so admirably established by his valuable experiments."
