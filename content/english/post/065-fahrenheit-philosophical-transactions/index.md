Volume XXXIII of *Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society* contains papers for the years 1724 and 1725. Among them are the first appearances of four papers by Daniel Fahrenheit. These papers were preceded by the only other paper Fahrenheit published, "Experimenta circa gradum caloris liquorum nonnullorum ebullientium instituta." For his work as an instrument-maker and the introduction of his temperature scale, Fahrenheit was elected as a Fellow of the Royal Society in 1724.

{{< figure src="title page.jpg" title="Title page of volume XXXIII of Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society" width="550" class="left">}}

The first paper in this volume is "Experimenta & observationes de congelatione aquae in vacuo factae." In it, Fahrenheit describes the results of his experiments with freezing water in a vacuum that he conducted in March of 1721. First, two kinds of thermometers were created, one filled with wine and the other with quicksilver (mercury). The thermometers went from 0 degrees to 98 degrees. The lowest part of the scale was marked by mixing ice, water, and sea salt. The thirtieth degree was marked by mixing ice and water and denotes when freezing begins. The ninety-sixth degree was defined as the temperature the thermometer reached when held in the mouth or under the armpits of a healthy person (i.e., someone without a fever). The figure below shows a glass globe with a tube two or three inches long. The globe is evacuated of air. Even in fifteen and twenty degrees, the water in the tube remained liquid. Fahrenheit does not offer any thoughts about these results because of the "insufficient amount of experiments."

{{< figure src="glass globe.jpg" title="Glass globe with a tube used by Fahrenheit to show the effects of atmospheric pressure on the boiling and freezing points of water" width="550" class="left">}}

The second paper is "Materiarum quarundam gravitates specificae diversis temporibus ad varios scopos exploratae," in which Fahrenheit studies the specific gravities of different materials. These values are given in a table at the beginning of the paper. The solids were first weighed in air using an accurate balance and then in rainwater. The specific gravities of liquids were measured using the two instruments shown in the figure below.

{{< figure src="two instruments.jpg" title="Two instruments used to determine the specific gravity of liquids" width="550" class="left">}}

The third paper is "Araeometri novi descriptio & usus," and describes a new hydrometer that was used to measure the specific gravity of liquids. The specific gravities of liquids can be measured by two methods, using scales or hydrometers. The issue with the former method is that very accurate scales must be used, and their accuracy can degrade over time. The issue with hydrometers is that the specific gravities of multiple liquids cannot be measured by the same instrument, and when liquids are changed, another hydrometer must be used. The figure below shows Fahrenheit's hydrometer, which could overcome the challenges posed by traditional hydrometers.

{{< figure src="hydrometer.jpg" title="Fahrenheit's hydrometer, which enables the specific gravity of multiple liquids to be measured by the same instrument" width="400" class="left">}}

The fourth and final paper is "Barometri novi descriptio." Fahrenheit states that he already mentioned the fact that water boils at a temperature of 212 degrees, though this value can change as the gravity of the atmosphere changes. While he holds off on discussing the results of these experiments, he mentions a special thermometer that can used to measure the atmospheric pressure just as well as a barometer. The figure below shows this thermometer. "To the cylinder AB is attached a tube BC, to which is added an oblong globe CD, and to this tube a very small opening DE is provided. The cylinder is filled with a certain liquid, which can carry the heat of boiling water. In the tube BC, the degrees of heat in the air are measured by means of the scale affixed to the tube. If, however, the thermometer is placed in boiling water, the liquid in the thermometer will not only fill the globe CD but also rise to the end of the tube DE based on the degree of heat which the water will acquire from the gravity of the atmosphere during the experiment."
