At the International Exposition of Electricity held in Paris between August 15 and November 15, 1881, Gaston Tissandier operated a small electric balloon powered by a Trouv&#233; motor. After this exhibition, Tissandier partnered with his brother Albert to open a balloon factory in Auteuil to construct a large, elongated balloon, which was used to make an ascent on October 8, 1883. "Tissandier's Electric Balloon" is a long article detailing the balloon, its construction, and the electric motor that powered it.

The balloon consisted of three parts: the balloon itself, the hydrogen apparatus to inflate it, and the electric motor that drove a screw to create propulsion. The balloon was constructed by Henri Lachambre, who was famous for working with aeronauts such as Salomon August Andr&#233;e Alberto Santos-Dumont. The balloon was 28 meters in length and 9.2 meters in diameter with a capacity of 1,060 cubic meters. Including all the various parts, the balloon weighed 1,240 kilograms.

{{< figure src="electric balloon oct 8.jpg" title="Tissandier's electric balloon, launched on October 8, 1883, at the moment of ascent" width="650" class="left">}}

The article gives details about the balloon construction and an account of the ascent in Tissandier's own words. The balloon was launched on October 8, 1883. Inflation of the balloon was done via equatorial cords. It began at eight in the morning and was done at half past two in the afternoon. The car, shown below, was in the shape of a cage and was constructed of bamboo strengthened by cords and copper wires.

Four batteries were used for this flight. The battery consisted of "a large trough containing six compartments; and each of the latter, forming a pile element, contains, mounted upon lead-coated copper rods, 11 thin carbons, and 10 zincs placed alternately along side of each other." The batteries were stored in the car in such a way as to occupy the least amount of space possible. The motor was made by Siemens and weighed 54 kilograms. It was made specially for this balloon in a shop in Paris.

{{< figure src="battery.jpg" title="One of the four batteries used by the balloon" width="550" class="left">}}

The ascent began at twenty minutes past three. Albert Tissandier was in charge of the ballast to ensure that the balloon remained at a steady height of 400 or 500 meters. When facing a wind that was blowing at three meters per second, the balloon was able to remain steady in the air. Unfortunately, when dealing with a headwind, there were gyratory motions that became worse over time. Using the rudder, the brothers were able to steer the balloon left and right while traveling in the direction of the wind. The balloon landed safely at thirty-five minutes past four. It was left inflated all night, and when they checked on it the following morning, it was found not to have lost any gas.

{{< figure src="electric balloon.jpg" title="Tissandier's electric balloon in flight" width="650" class="left">}}

Tissandier concluded that "electricity furnishes a balloon with one of the most favorable of motors, the management of which in the car is extremely easy." He added that this was a preliminary trial and that a further trials with better materials and a more powerful motor would be performed in the future.
