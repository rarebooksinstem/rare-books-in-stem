This article in *Nature* describes "experiments in photographic a&#235;rostation" that took place on June 19, 1885. The two people onboard the balloon were Gaston Tissandier and Jacques Ducom, who was responsible for operating the photography apparatus. The ascent began at forty minutes past one in the afternoon. The first two photographs were taken at an altitude of 670 meters. The first figure below shows a reproduction by heliogravure of a photograph taken above Paris from an altitude of 605 meters. Though some of the finer details of the photograph are missing from this reproduction, it is still possible to see things like people walking by below.

Before leaving Paris, two more photographs were taken at altitudes of 800 and 820 meters, for a total of five photographs between 1:40 and 2:12 p.m., during which time the balloon traveled 11 kilometers. Another two photographs were taken at altitudes of 1000 and 1100 meters outside the city. The article notes that having two or three photography apparatus with their own operator would allow for a series of photographs to be taken while in the air. There are also details of what the ideal conditions are for taking aerial photographs. The article ends by stating the advantages of aerial photography for military and meteorological applications.
