This short article discusses the possibility of using electric batteries to propel balloons. The author of the article notes that Martin Farquhar Tupper, who was a famous author of his time and also gave lectures on ballooning, suggested using the storage battery invented by Camille Alphonse Faure. This idea was something Gaston Tissandier already presented at a meeting of the French Academy of Sciences. The author writes, "The true solution of the problem, if it be feasible at all, appears to us to lie not in the exclusive use of levitation or electric power, but in a proper combination of both principles. This plan is that which M. Tissandier contemplates, and he points out that a propeller driven by electricity possesses advantages over other modes of movement. For example, it requires no fire, which is a dangerous element in a balloon inflated with hydrogen gas; it has a constant weight and gives off no products of combustion, and is readily manipulated."

To show the usefulness of batteries, Tissandier created a small balloon 11 feet in length and 4.5 feet in diameter, with a volume of 484 gallons. A "Trouv&#233; motor of the Siemens type" with the help with a Plant&#233; battery was able to drive an 18-inch-diameter, double-bladed screw 6.5 turns per second and propel the balloon through the air at a speed of over three feet per second. With more elements, the speed of the balloon was increased. The author says that the it is possible to achieve speeds of 12 to 15 miles per hour in calm weather using a larger setup of a dynamo-electric motor and batteries. While this speed can only be maintained for a limited time, using lighter batteries and Faure's accumulators can help sustain these speeds for a longer period.
